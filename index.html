Repair and Return

Phantom limb pain is more than a physical sensation; it is an embodied trauma, where the body remembers what has been lost. Similarly, the act of digging tunnels in Underground Gaza represents a collective response to the forced amputation of land, history, and community. Both phenomena desire to reconnect one within the embodied memories of nerve system, the other within the land, culture and home. Just as the body experiences pain in an absent limb, displaced communities instinctively carve pathways to reclaim their existence and familiarities they used to have. These acts reflect resilience, resistance, and the persistence of memory.

Phantom Limb Pain

"Memory for Pain. The amputation of a limb represents a serious disruption of body integrity and is associated with a number of negative consequences, particularly disability and postamputation pain. (Fuchs et.al, 2018, p. 1)"

"Several theories of chronic pain suggest that both declarative and nondeclarative memory processes contribute to the development and maintenance of chronic pain via neuroplastic changes in the nervous system [81–83]. One example for this process is central sensitization, referring to a hyperexcitability of the central nervous system and associated lowered pain thresholds [84]. In different groups with chronic pain, structural and functional reorganization within pain-processing brain areas has been shown, which might represent a neural correlate of pain memory [81, 85, 86].

Katz and Melzack [61] and Katz [87] proposed a crucial role of somatosensory memories for both PLP and nonpainful phantom phenomena (e.g., phantom limb awareness or phantom sensations). Anderson-Barnes et al. [88] suggested that phantom sensations could be explained by proprioceptive memories and that they might become associated with pain perceived before the amputation by means of learning mechanisms.

Melzack [61] suggested that pain prior to the amputation is encoded and can later be triggered, for example, by peripheral input stemming from the residual limb. This triggered preamputation pain is then experienced as PLP. Maladaptive plasticity that is positively correlated with PLP [65] can be seen as neural underpinning of pain memory in amputees. These processes might be more severe if chronic pain has already been present prior to amputation [81, 85, 86], potentially influencing the formation of a pain memory.

The theory of a relationship between pre amputation pain (that leaves behind a memory trace) and PLP is supported by retrospective reports that have shown similarities between memories referring to somatosensory perceptions in the affected limb in the phase before amputation and later phantom sensations [60, 61].
(Fuchs et.al, 2018, p. 5)

The phenomenon of phantom limb pain, the lingering sensation of an absent limb, is not merely a neurological response to physical amputation but also a deeply embodied trauma, where the body and mind retain the memory of what was once there.

This pain could extends beyond the individual, metaphorically resonating within entire communities that have experienced forced separations from their familiarities. Similarly, the act of digging tunnels in Gaza emerges as a collective response to a different kind of amputation within land, history, culture, and freedom.

The experience of phantom limb pain is also an act of endless searching: a deep, unconscious desire to reconnect with the amputated part of the body, as if it were still there.

This mirrors the act of digging, where the bodily act itself embodies an intense desire to find something lost.

Digging is more than a physical act, it could also be a metaphor for searching, or even investigating, an expression that drives individuals and communities to reach deep into the ground, memory, or self in pursuit of what has been missing.

Whether felt in the body or enacted in the landscape, both phenomena reveal an instinctive resistance to loss—a refusal to accept absence as final.
